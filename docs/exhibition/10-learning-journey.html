Our Learning Journey

“The story of how you got there — photos, sketches, notes, false starts and breakthroughs — helps others understand the richness of your learning journey.”

Phase 1 — Investigate
What did we investigate and why?
Our question: “What can kids create when they have professional tools and an AI engineering partner?”

We wanted to find out if four kids (ages 9–11) could design and build a real video game — not a tutorial or a template, but an original game with our own characters, levels, bosses, and story. We used Claude (an AI) as our engineer: we told it what to build, and it wrote the code.
Evidence: Game concept document, design PRDs, artist brief for Andrew
"Kids can design a game that can be extremely successful since kids play games themselves and have great taste in games."
— Eins, 11
Phase 2 — Discover
What did we learn?
Game design is harder than coding. Coming up with good ideas was the easy part. Making them work together — balanced difficulty, controls that feel right, levels that teach without tutorials — took real thought and lots of testing.

Communication IS the skill. The AI can write any code, but only if you describe what you want clearly. Learning to say “the jump feels too floaty” instead of “it’s broken” was the biggest skill we developed.

Iteration makes everything better. Nothing was right on the first try. We played, found problems, described them, watched them get fixed, and repeated — dozens of times. Each loop made the game better.
Evidence: Build Record sheets, bug lists, gameplay tuning logs, overnight AI test results
"How quickly Claude could understand us and create a functioning game, even if it isn't perfect."
— Andrew, 12
Phase 3 — Reflect
How did we learn it?
We followed a 5-session process: Dream → Design → Build → Test → Ship.

In every session, we talked to the AI like a teammate. We used voice (Wispr Flow) to explain our ideas naturally. We played the game after every change and gave honest feedback. We rotated roles so everyone got to play, report bugs, and direct the AI.

The most powerful moment: watching our words change the code. One kid says “the ground pound should hit more enemies,” Claude changes a number, we restart the game, and it’s different. That feedback loop — speak, change, test — is how we learned everything.
Evidence: Learning reflections, process photos, session recordings
"You need context. If you just say 'make a game', then it wouldn't know what game to make or how to make it."
— Ethan, 9
Phase 4 — Share
What are we sharing today?
A real, playable game — 15 floors, 5 bosses, original sound and music, running on a Raspberry Pi with a Nintendo controller.

And the story of how we built it — 4 kids who had never made a game before, working with AI, learning that the hardest part isn’t the technology. It’s knowing what you want and finding the right words to ask for it.
