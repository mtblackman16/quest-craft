Showcase — The Final Act
March 22, 2026  |  Ethan, Eins, Andrew & Nathan
Quiz show, interviews, and building the exhibition
Today the team became presenters.
Four kids who spent a month designing a video game sat down, answered every question thrown at them, and proved they understood not just what they built — but how and why they built it.
v1.0
Code freeze — the game is officially finished

What They Did Today

  • Quiz Show (5 rounds) — Game design, AI engineering, build process, lightning round, and secret-hunting. They answered questions about PRDs, context windows, sub-agents, Git, and all 5 bosses. Every round: passed.
  • Team Interview (3 acts, 17+ questions) — Delivered the elevator pitch, told the story of SPLIT from origin to final build, and each kid gave individual reflections on what they learned and how.
  • Board building — Assembled the physical display board with printed materials, quote cards, and game screenshots.
  • Print materials — Generated reflection cards, team quote sheets, demo cheat sheet, and learning journey updates from real interview quotes.

Skills Unlocked

Metacognition & Reflection
Articulated not just what they learned, but how they learned it. Connecting experiences to insights is a skill that transfers to every subject.
Exhibition Design
Designed a physical display that tells a story: combining printed materials, original artwork, handwritten elements, and photos into a cohesive narrative about their process.
Public Speaking & Demo Skills
Practiced explaining complex work to strangers. The ability to stand in front of your work and say “here’s what we did and why it matters” is a lifelong skill.
Shipping a Product
Experienced the full arc from idea to finished product: Dream → Design → Build → Test → Ship. Most adults never finish a project this completely. They did it in 5 sessions.

In Their Own Words

Eins: “Kids can design a game that can be extremely successful since kids play games themselves and have great taste in games.”
Andrew: “I should not worry so much about how the designs that I make turn out, and that I should just go with the flow.”
Ethan: “You need context. If you just say ‘make a game’, then it wouldn’t know what game to make or how to make it.”
Nathan: “Even if the Raspberry Pi breaks, you can use GitHub to still have the code.”
Team: “Sub-agents are like making somebody else do a task so you focus on the main thing.”

Andrew: “How quickly Claude could understand us and create a functioning game, even if it isn’t perfect.”
Team: “Jello feels kid-friendly and fun, and we were thinking about something that would be simple to create and yet be kind of memorable.”
How This Maps to LASD Illuminate: This session completed all four Illuminate phases. Phase 1 (investigate): their inquiry question and plan have been clear since Session 1. Phase 2 (discover): Build Records from Session 4 show iterative learning. Phase 3 (reflect): today’s Learning Stories capture deep, structured reflection on what they learned and how. Phase 4 (share): the display board, the live game demo, and practiced presentation skills. From “What if kids could build a game with AI?” to “Here it is — play it yourself” in 5 sessions.

The Full Journey

  • Session 1 — Dream: Brainstormed the game concept, named it SPLIT, watched AI generate a playable demo in 10 minutes
  • Session 2 — Design: Designed every detail across 7 documents, integrated Andrew’s artwork into game assets
  • Between sessions: Claude built the complete game from their designs — 15 floors, 5 bosses, ~13,000 lines of code
  • Session 4 — Playtest: Found and fixed dozens of bugs, tuned the game feel, documented the process, set up AI testing
  • Session 5 — Showcase: Built the display board, wrote learning stories, practiced the demo, froze the code at v1.0
Five sessions. Four kids. One game. One story. They didn’t just build a game — they learned how to learn with AI.
