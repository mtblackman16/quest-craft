Every character, enemy, and item in SPLIT started as Andrew's original digital artwork. During Session 2, Andrew designed the full visual identity of the game: the jello cube hero, the Sanitizer Warrior enemies, the hand sanitizer and jelly powder items, and the defeated enemy states. These drawings became the reference material that guided the game's pixel art, animations, and visual style across all 15 floors.

The Hero

Jello Cube — The Player Character
Two views of the jello cube hero (front and three-quarter perspective) alongside the Jelly Powder bag, the in-game collectible that powers up the player. Andrew designed the cube to look translucent and wobbly, capturing the feel the team wanted from the very first brainstorm.

Items

Hand Sanitizer — Enemy Weapon / Collectible Item
Front and back views of the hand sanitizer bottle. The front label reads "Germ B Gone" with a no-germs symbol. The back has a full ingredients panel. This became both the weapon carried by Sanitizer Warriors and a dropped item players can collect.
Dropped Items — Defeated Enemy Loot
When a Sanitizer Warrior is defeated, it drops its equipment in a puddle of spilled sanitizer. Andrew designed the fallen hand sanitizer bottle and jelly powder bag sitting in the purple pool, which became the in-game item drop animation.

The Sanitizer Warrior

Design note: Andrew created the Sanitizer Warrior as the game's primary enemy. It's a purple-skinned soldier wearing brown pants and a spiked metal helmet, carrying a hose-connected spray weapon fed by a hand sanitizer tank on its back. The kids chose this enemy because it's the natural opposite of a jello cube: sanitizer destroys germs, and jello is squishy and vulnerable.

From Drawing to Game

How it worked: Andrew drew all of these during Session 2 (Design). The team uploaded his artwork into the project's asset folder, and Claude used them as reference to generate the pixel art sprites, idle animations, attack patterns, and death sequences for the final game. Every enemy encounter across 15 floors traces back to these seven drawings.
